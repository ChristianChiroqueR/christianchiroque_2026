Spotting suspicious patterns with decision trees

A walk through how interpretable models can support AML/CFT analysis without losing statistical rigor.

In financial intelligence analysis, interpretability isn't a luxury — it's a requirement. A model that flags a transaction as suspicious but can't explain why simply isn't useful as the basis for a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR).

So before jumping to deep neural networks, it pays to explore what a good decision tree can deliver. In this post I walk through a reproducible R pipeline combining tidymodels, rpart, and class-balancing techniques to build an interpretable classifier.

The problem

FIUs receive thousands of reports per month. Manual prioritization isn't feasible. The question is: can we build a risk score that's defensible in court?

Pipeline

  1. Cleaning and feature engineering with recipes.
  2. Tree training with stratified cross-validation.
  3. Variable importance analysis and local SHAP.
  4. Evaluation with AUC, minority-class recall, and confusion matrix.
A well-pruned tree with 12 terminal nodes reached an AUC of 0.89 — comparable to a random forest, but auditable.

Lessons

Interpretability forces you to understand the domain. And understanding the domain improves the model. When the financial analyst can read the tree's rules, they spot patterns the model hadn't quite captured, and that feeds back into the next iteration.
